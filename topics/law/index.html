Topic 4: Law

Next we turn to when, whether, and why are compelled to obey laws.

Reading

  1. Plato, Crito (early 4th century BCE), translated by Cathal Woods and Ryan Pack (2012), available from SSRN, or this alternative version. Read pages 1, 2, and 7–9 of this 10-page text (the rest is optional).
  2. Martin Luther King Jr., “Letter from Birmingham Jail,”1 (1963), available here. Consider reading the whole thing, but our discussion will concern paragraphs 9–18 (from “You may well ask: ‘Why direct action?‘” to “that country’s antireligious laws”). So read those paragraphs with care.

See also

  • Read more about Socrates and what we know about him here in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

  • Those of you who attended school in the US probably won’t need an introduction to King, but not everyone is in that category. His Wikipedia bio seems well done.
  • King refers to Thomas Aquinas’s account of laws; it’s in Aquinas’s Summa Theologiae in Part 1–2, especially Questions 90–95. Here, for example is part of Question 92, Article 1, Reply to Objection 4: “Since a tyrannical law is not in accord with reason, it is not a law absolutely speaking, but is instead a kind of perversion of law.” See Freddosso’s translation online. Aquinas is perhaps the most important theologian for Roman Catholic Christianity.
  • King refers to Jewish philosopher Martin Buber’s book I and Thou (1923) without elaborating; here’s a brief excerpt from the book. See the SEP entry on Buber for more, though it’s not written at an introductory level.
