Topic 5: Knowing and Being

Here we meet skepticism, the worry that we can't have genuine knowledge, and then accounts of how we can have knowledge. This introduces us to Epistemology. We then see how epistemology connects with Metaphysics, in seeing how accounts of how we know are related to accounts of what we are. We'll do this by focusing on two early modern philosophers and some their critics, past and present.

Reading

  • René Descartes, “First Meditation and Second Meditation” from Meditations on First Philosophy in Which Are Demonstrated the Existence of God and the Distinction Between the Human Soul and Body. ed. Jonathan Bennett.1 You can also download or listen to an audio version (.mp3 format) of this text.
  • Elisabeth of Bohemia, letters to Descartes. Read letters 6.v.1643 (starts on page 1) and 10.vi.1643 (starts on page 4). total: about 3 pages.
  • John Locke, excerpts from Enquiry concerning Human Understanding (1690)
    • from Book 2, Chapter i “Ideas in general, and their origin,” pages 18–20;
    • from Book 2, Chapter xxvii “Identity and diversity,” pages 112–121.
  • Marya Schechtman, from “The Same and the Same” (1994) American Philosophical Quarterly 31(3): 199–212 (14 pages, focus on the first two pages 199–200, and then 207–210) (via Axinn Library login)
  • W.E.B. DuBois “Strivings of the Negro People” from The Atlantic (1897), or try here, if you have trouble with that link; read up through “The Lord has bought your liberty!”
  • Jorge Luis Borges, “Borges and I” (originally published in Spanish as “Borges y Yo”).
  • “The Lost Mariner” by Oliver Sacks, from The New York Review of Books (1984)

See also

  • Peter Adamson accessibly discusses other historical skeptics, before Descartes, like Nagarjuna, Nicholas of Autrecourt, and Sextus Empiricus. He also has several podcast episodes (.mp3) on Nagarjuna on the History of Philosophy without Gaps podcast (which you can also find wherever you get podcasts). Also: Sextus Empiricus (.mp3).
  • Thinking about optical illusions (nice collection of examples) is a great way to appreciate the idea that we only believe illusions are illusory because our senses are mostly trustworthy. My favorites are probably motion-induced blindness illusions.
  • SEP article on the life and philosophy of Elisabeth, Princess of Bohemia and the Project Vox profile page on Princess Elisabeth.
  • DuBois's “Double Consciousness” in the SEP

  1. You can read more about how this text has been modified by the editor to bring it closer to contemporary English than many translations do, while preserving and highlighting the structure of its reasoning.
